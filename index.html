In my own words
Most large public programs fail the people they were designed to serve. Not from lack of funding or intent, but from structural misdiagnosis and broken accountability. Over six years, I have worked inside those systems: mapping root causes, building the stakeholder coalitions that make alignment stick, and turning field evidence into governance frameworks that senior leadership can actually act on. My edge is the combination: hypothesis-driven problem structuring, Lean Six Sigma rigour, and the execution discipline to stay until the numbers move for the communities at the end of the pipeline. Women-led SHGs in Odisha. Out-of-school children in Kalahandi. Informal micro-entrepreneurs across five states. That is who the work is for.

Four Problems I Worked On

Context
Nuapada had fragmented skilling supply with no shared accountability. Departments, industry bodies, and training institutions all operated independently with no common outcome framework.
Problem
Funding for a regional hub required demonstrated cross-departmental commitment, but no mechanism existed to align stakeholders. Programs overlapped without coordination, making measurable impact impossible.
What I did
Ran a district-wide diagnostic using stakeholder interviews and process mapping to identify root causes, not symptoms, of the placement gap. Built an issue tree separating supply-side failures (training quality, mobilisation reach) from demand-side misalignment (industry engagement, job-readiness standards). Structured a coalition across 25+ government departments, industry partners, and training institutions around a shared outcome framework with named accountability. Prepared decision memos, approval notes, and real-time MIS dashboards so state leadership could act on evidence rather than anecdote.
What happened
₹8 crore secured for the regional skilling hub. Interventions reached 1,000+ beneficiaries , primarily first-generation job seekers and women from tribal communities in one of Odisha's most underserved districts , with a 30% improvement in mobilisation efficiency. Supported 500+ enrolments and 40 Nano Unicorn enterprise applications, creating formal livelihoods where none previously existed.
What I learned
Funding follows demonstrated alignment. I stopped thinking about the money and focused entirely on building the coalition. Once 25 stakeholders were already committed and accountable to the same outcome, the funding followed naturally.
Context
Rural micro-enterprises across Rajasthan, UP, Delhi-NCR, and Haryana lacked formal registration, digital market access, and welfare entitlements. Millions of livelihoods sat outside the formal economy.
Problem
There was no standardised model for identifying, onboarding, and formalising MSMEs at scale while simultaneously linking them to corporate platforms, welfare schemes, and government programs across five states in parallel.
What I did
Mapped the full citizen-to-entitlement journey across five states to identify where informal micro-enterprises fell through. Hypothesis: the bottleneck was not awareness but access architecture: no single point of intake, verification, and onboarding. Designed a citizen-centric welfare scheme repository through primary research and field verification, then digitised it into Haqdarshak's platform. Built field-team training protocols and performance tracking across concurrent state operations. Partners included Flipkart, HUL, and Tata Power.
What happened
Onboarded 2,100 businesses under Flipkart Samarth, scaled to 20,000 MSMEs with HUL, and enabled 13,500 citizens , the majority women-led informal enterprises , to access welfare entitlements with Tata Power. Across five states. At the same time.
What I learned
Scale requires a platform mindset, not a program mindset. Standardise the intake, digitise the repository, train the field layer once. After that, the system delivers itself across geographies without you managing every district individually.
Context
Across 1.14 lakh government schools in Madhya Pradesh, 4.36 lakh teachers were managing 46 administrative registers, consuming over 80 days per teacher per year in paperwork alone.
Problem
Poor learning outcomes were blamed on poor teaching quality. The actual problem was structural: administrative compliance physically prevented teachers from spending time in classrooms. The symptom was visible. The cause was not.
What I did
Applied Lean Six Sigma methodology , value stream mapping, root cause analysis, and process rationalisation , to the full data management workflow across the school system. Hypothesis: administrative redundancy, not teaching quality, was the binding constraint on learning outcomes. Interviewed 40+ teachers, headmasters, and district officials. Identified 26 redundant data points across 46 registers. Co-designed a rationalised framework with district administrators and secured state leadership sign-off for pilot rollout.
What happened
Headmaster burden dropped from 46 to 20 days annually. Teacher burden from 80 to 40 days. 50% cut in administrative time across 4.36 lakh teachers in 1.14 lakh schools. The reclaimed instructional hours translated directly into improved student learning outcomes , without adding a single new teacher, rupee, or program layer to the system.
What I learned
When systems underperform, the visible problem is rarely the real one. The fix was not more teachers. It was removing what was stopping the existing ones from doing their jobs. Good diagnosis is worth more than any intervention designed on assumptions.
Context
COVID-19 pulled 13,998 students in Kalahandi district out of school. Most were absorbed into household labour. School officials had no systematic protocol for finding and re-enrolling them at scale.
Problem
Education officials wanted to reach families but were buried in compliance work. The bottleneck was not motivation. It was the system consuming every available hour before any outreach could happen.
What I did
Ran a rapid diagnostic with district administration to identify where re-integration was failing. Root cause: officials had motivation but no time. Compliance workload consumed all available capacity before any outreach could happen. Designed a distributed re-integration system at block, cluster, and school levels with clear ownership at each tier. Trained CRCCs and Headmasters on the protocol. Streamlined digital reporting to recover educator hours and redirecting that recovered time toward the only thing that worked: personally reaching families and counselling children back to school.
What happened
Over 75% of 13,998 out-of-school children aged 6 to 14 were re-enrolled , children at highest risk of permanent dropout, child labour, and early marriage. No additional staff. No new budget line. A system intervention restored the right to education for over 10,000 children without any new resources entering the system.
What I learned
Capacity is often latent, not absent. The educators had everything they needed except the time. Once the administrative burden lifted, the results came from the people already inside the system. Remove the friction and trust the people.

How I Think

Four things I keep coming back to

Diagnose before you prescribe
Most development programs fail because someone designed a solution for the wrong problem. I build issue trees and run structured diagnostics before writing a single recommendation. It feels slow. It prevents expensive, well-funded mistakes.
Alignment is not a soft skill , it is the work
In government systems, getting 25 departments to commit to the same outcome framework and stay accountable to it is the hardest and highest-value thing you can do. A transformation roadmap without that alignment is just a document.
Measure what matters to the person at the end of the pipeline
Every intervention I design has an outcome defined at the start. Not to satisfy donors, but because vague metrics are how programs drift from the communities they were built to serve. Output counts are not outcomes.
Technology should restore rights, not just improve efficiency
The welfare repository I built was not about digitisation. It was about a citizen's right to know what the government had already committed to give them. That distinction between empowerment and efficiency changes every design decision that follows.
